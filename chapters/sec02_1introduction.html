Our Future,The Way We See It

Introduction
Marissa Jansz
Scroll or drag to navigate

Although we know we cannot predict the future with a hundred per cent accuracy, it does not stop us from trying. While there are many who find amusement in trying to imagine what the world will look like in the decades ahead, there are others for whom making projections is serious business. The predictions of the latter group are used by organisations and individuals to lay the groundwork for potential future changes and be ready to face what is to come.

One of the core aims of the training given to Gateway students is to prepare them for the future. As part of this endeavour and in commemoration of its twenty-fifth anniversary, the school launched a programme to encourage students to visualise the future using history and the present as their yardsticks. Students could select one of fifteen categories and, working in groups, produce their imaginings of how things will be in that specific field in twenty-five years. They were free to choose any mode they wanted, be it an essay, drawing, photography, website, invention, song, poetry – absolutely anything that they were comfortable with. The main requirement was that they keep their ideas grounded in past trends and the present reality.

This programme was kicked-off with an online seminar conducted by data scientist and science fiction writer Yudhanjaya Wijeratne, who introduced the students to making scientific and data-based predictions. He took them through the process of observing historical trends and using contextual detail to suggest potential changes in the future, providing relatable examples to illustrate how to arrive at conclusions.

The response from the students was mind-blowing!

There were over two hundred and fifty submissions. There were so many varied versions of the future world and it was clear that the students had invested a lot of time, thought and effort into their projects. The editorial team had a mammoth task in selecting the entries to be featured in this book. The goal was to present the most creative, innovative and plausible predictions of the future while also covering as many of the categories and as wide an array of modes as possible – and in doing so, offer a glimpse into how these young people interpret their current reality as well as their hopes and aspirations for the next quarter of a century.

A note on the presentation of material. The text-based projects have been featured in their entirety. All multi-media projects have an introduction along with a few photos and/or screenshots as a preview, with the full projects available on a microsite through the provided link. While there has been some discreet editing of projects, care was taken to retain authenticity and maintain the voice of the students.
