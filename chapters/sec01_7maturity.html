Chapter 7:

Maturity

Promoting experiential and independent learning.

Gateway College celebrated its sixteenth anniversary in 2013. The milestone was bittersweet; Mr Ralph Alles, whose visits to the schools had grown more infrequent as his health declined, passed away in November of that year. Gateway was, by this time, a prominent feature on the national education landscape, with four branches in different parts of the country, an aggregate student body of about 2000 and thousands of alumni. Only sixteen years old at this time – old enough to have proven its stability, yet a mere infant in comparison to Sri Lanka’s oldest schools – it was still growing rapidly. A well-founded reputation for excellence saw to this. The school’s founder had left behind a proud and remarkable legacy. It was now up to his family – his immediate family and his Gateway one – to carry the school forward into its next era.

The image and ethos of the school continued to evolve. That year, the College motto was changed; henceforth, it would be ‘Gateway to Learning’ rather than ‘Gateway to Knowledge’. There was much more to this than the mere alteration of a word; the change was an acknowledgement that the ultimate purpose of learning is not knowledge but wisdom, not expertise but understanding and responsibility. Growing out of values that had always been precious to its founder, the change of motto signified a new phase in Gateway’s evolution, a renewal of commitment to the building of character.

This change of mission and outlook demanded outer as well as inner expression. The school uniform underwent a comprehensive redesign to reflect it. In place of the old light-blue shirts featuring the gates-and-lamp device and the old motto, a new uniform shirt in periwinkle blue was introduced, carrying the new crest and ‘Gateway to Learning’ motto on the breast pocket. A new tie and school scarf were also introduced for use on appropriate occasions. Subsequently, a midnight-blue jacket was made mandatory for A-level students at assembly, formal school events and when entering or leaving the school premises. The bottom half of the uniform, a grey skirt for girls and matching trousers for boys, remains unchanged.

The new uniform and blazer for A-level students.

Diverse offerings for diverse learning styles

Emphasis on learning rather than knowledge (or academic results) is manifested more practically by making a wider range of academic and vocational subjects available to Gateway pupils. On the academic side, there are regular subject classes with optional emphasis on the STEM (science, technology, engineering, mathematics) cluster. Pupils also learn in a more hands-on way when exploring vocational subjects such as robotics, software application development, AI, and working in the schools’ Innovation Labs. Meanwhile, they broaden their understanding through an interdisciplinary approach in which various school departments collaborate to teach a single subject from multiple perspectives. Gateway kids learn important professional and life skills such as analytical and critical thinking, research and writing. Some pupils follow diploma courses in business, computing and technology and even drama (LAMDA), and others pursue vocational programmes (BTEC) in music technology, sports and engineering. All students are encouraged to cultivate their interests and are given many options to help them turn a passion into a profession.

On the cusp of celebrating its silver anniversary in 2022, Gateway College signed a collaborative partnership with SACE International (SACEi), allowing pupils the option of following South Australian high-school curricula as an alternative to the UK-based IGCSE and A-level programme. SACEi’s flexible, highly student-centric approach gives continuous assessment, rather than examinations, a key role in the award of qualifications. Pupils (or their parents) are free to select which curriculum they follow.
