A Work in Progress

The Business of Education
Scroll or drag to navigate

‘Education in Ceylon,’ the great Sri Lankan journalist Herbert Hulugalle once remarked, ‘is big business.’ Published in the 1950s, Hulugalle’s words are as true in Sri Lanka today as when they were written.

He did not add – for in his time, it hardly needed to be said – that the education so prized was always given in English. Under British rule, a two-track system of instruction had evolved in Ceylon. Modern education in the ‘vernacular media’ – Sinhala and Tamil – could be had, free of charge, at any government or Christian missionary-run primary school. But undervalued and marginalised as it was, this education was often of poor quality, culturally insular, and ended when the pupil was aged twelve or thirteen. As a preparation for life in Britain’s showpiece ‘premier Crown Colony,’ it left much to be desired. Meanwhile, however, modern, high-quality, culturally cosmopolitan education, delivered in English, was also available from Christian mission schools, schools run by Buddhist, Hindu and Muslim social organisations and a few commercial providers, as well as just one state school, Royal College – but only to those who could afford to pay for it.

A series of education reforms, commencing in 1939, attempted to remedy this inequity by abolishing private education as well as education delivered in English. Successive governments devoted vast amounts of money, energy and effort to this project, yet it never quite succeeded. Suppressing English did not improve the quality of vernacular education in Ceylon. Nor did it dissuade Sri Lankans from continuing to spend as much as they could afford to teach their children the language, for English was and remains the key to social and financial advancement in Sri Lanka. Classes in ‘spoken English’ as well as O-level and A-level English mushroomed all over the country despite the ban on private education. A massive ‘private tuition’ industry grew up to make up for this and other shortfalls in the state system. Finally, in the late seventies, the state accepted the realities of the situation and began to permit both private and English education again.

In the meantime, social inequities caused by the problems of the national system, combined with the pressure of a growing population, had brought the frustration of young Sri Lankans to boiling-point. The armed youth who attempted to take over the country in 1971 were secondary-school leavers led by university students who had found that, despite their degrees, jobs were scarce. Other aspects of the national system inadvertently gave rise to estrangement and suspicion among the different races and faiths of multicultural Sri Lanka, with tragic consequences that began soon after independence and have been manifested repeatedly through much of our recent history.

When a similar uprising occurred at the end of the 1980s, with the country already in the midst of a youth-led ethnic conflict, it was clear that the state, on its own, was unable to deal with the problems of national education. It was time for others – able, experienced educationists with genuine concern for the welfare of future generations – to take a hand. Thanks to the regulatory changes of the late seventies, this was once again possible. And so education in Sri Lanka became not just big business, but serious, patriotic business.
